Why Your LinkedIn Feed Feels Broken

The problem isn’t just that the algorithm is “bad”. The problem is that LinkedIn is trying to do several things at the same time:

  • keep you on the platform as long as possible,
  • show you content that creates engagement,
  • serve ads and promoted posts that pay the bills,
  • occasionally surface posts from people you actually care about.

In practice, this means your feed mixes:

  • posts from people you follow,
  • posts they liked or commented on,
  • suggested posts based on topics and trends,
  • promoted posts and ads.

When all of that gets blended together, your feed can start to feel noisy and random — especially if you’re using LinkedIn primarily for focused, professional work.

How the LinkedIn Algorithm Works in 2026 (Simplified)

You don’t need to know every technical detail, but understanding the basics helps you “train” the algorithm.

In very simple terms, the LinkedIn algorithm looks at:

  • Who you interact with (likes, comments, shares, DMs),
  • What topics you engage with (tech, HR, sales, AI, etc.),
  • What content keeps you scrolling (time on post, dwell time),
  • Which posts are performing well globally (viral content),
  • Which ads and promoted posts are targeting your profile.

The more you engage with a type of content, the more the algorithm assumes you want similar content. This can work in your favor — or completely backfire.

Accidentally like a few low-quality posts? Comment on a random debate? Click on a clickbait article?
Suddenly your feed gets flooded with more of the same.

Can You “Reset” the LinkedIn Algorithm?

There is no official “reset feed” button on LinkedIn.

But you can influence the algorithm in a few ways:

  • clean up who you follow,
  • stop engaging with the content you don’t want more of,
  • use LinkedIn settings to reduce certain types of noise,
  • and use tools that filter the feed for you.

The key idea is:

Stop feeding the algorithm with the wrong signals — and start sending the right ones.

Step 1 — Unfollow the Right People (Without Disconnecting)

One of the fastest ways to fix your LinkedIn feed is to unfollow accounts that constantly push low-value content.

Important: unfollowing is not the same as removing a connection.

When you unfollow someone:

  • you stay connected,
  • you can still DM each other,
  • you simply stop seeing their posts in your feed.

Start with:

  • people who post or react to drama constantly,
  • accounts that share vague motivational posts every day,
  • companies that post irrelevant updates for your career.

This alone can make your feed feel more focused and less chaotic.

Step 2 — Hide Posts You Don’t Want More Of

Every time you see a post that makes you think “Why is this here?”, that’s an opportunity to train the algorithm.

On any post, click the three-dot menu and choose:

  • “I don’t want to see this”, or
  • “Show less posts like this” (if available).

It feels small, but over time these signals add up.

The opposite is also true: if you scroll past content you actually like without engaging, LinkedIn gets a weak signal. Don’t be afraid to like and comment on posts that are genuinely valuable to you.

Step 3 — Be Intentional With What You Engage With

Think of your engagement as voting.

Every like, comment or share is a vote that says:

“More content like this, please.”

If you engage with:

  • outrage posts,
  • pointless debates,
  • clickbait threads,

… the algorithm will give you more of those.

Instead, try to intentionally engage with posts that match how you want your feed to look:

  • deep dives in your industry,
  • practical career advice,
  • case studies and breakdowns,
  • technical content that makes you better at your job.

This is one of the most underrated ways to “fix” your LinkedIn algorithm over time.

Step 4 — Tidy Up Notifications and Follows

Another reason your LinkedIn experience feels overwhelming is notification overload.

When every like, comment and new post triggers a notification, you get pulled into content you don’t really care about.

To fix this:

  • Go to Settings > Communications and turn off non-essential notifications.
  • Stop following irrelevant hashtags and topics.
  • Unsubscribe from notification emails you never open.

The fewer distractions you allow, the easier it is to use LinkedIn intentionally instead of reactively.

How do I fix my LinkedIn algorithm in 2026?

Why is my LinkedIn feed full of random posts?

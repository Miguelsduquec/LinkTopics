Why LinkedIn Shows Recommended Posts (And How to Reduce Them)

Why LinkedIn recommends content you didn’t ask for — and what control you realistically have.

Recommended posts are one of the most confusing parts of the LinkedIn feed.

They appear without a clear connection to people you follow, often interrupting otherwise relevant updates.

For many users, this creates the feeling that the feed is being driven by something opaque and hard to influence.

How LinkedIn Decides What to Recommend

LinkedIn uses a mix of behavioral signals to generate recommendations.

These include:

  • posts similar to ones you’ve engaged with,
  • topics popular within your broader network,
  • content performing well across the platform.

The goal is engagement, not precision.

Why Recommendations Often Miss the Mark

Recommendation systems work at scale.

That means they rely on patterns, not context.

As a result, recommended posts often:

  • surface topics you explored once but no longer care about,
  • favor viral content over useful updates,
  • repeat similar ideas across multiple posts.

The Cost of Constant Recommendations

Individually, a recommended post is easy to ignore.

Over time, however, they change how the feed feels.

Common effects include:

  • more scrolling to find relevant content,
  • less trust in what appears organically,
  • reduced sense of control.

What LinkedIn Lets You Control

LinkedIn offers limited ways to influence recommendations.

You can:

  • hide individual recommended posts,
  • reduce engagement with irrelevant topics,
  • unfollow sources that trigger repeated recommendations.

LinkedIn documents these controls here: LinkedIn Help — Hide and mute feed content

These actions help, but they don’t stop recommendations entirely.

Why Manual Control Isn’t Enough

Recommendations are continuously recalculated.

Even if you hide several posts today, new recommendations appear tomorrow.

Recommended posts are designed to keep you scrolling.

They are not designed to reflect your priorities.

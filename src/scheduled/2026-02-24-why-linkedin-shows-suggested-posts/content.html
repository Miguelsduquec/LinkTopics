Why LinkedIn Shows Suggested Posts (And How to Reduce Them)

Why suggested content appears in your feed — and what control you realistically have.

Suggested posts are often the most confusing elements in the LinkedIn feed.

They appear without a clear connection to people you follow or topics you intentionally chose.

For many users, this creates the impression that the feed is increasingly driven by exploration rather than relevance.

What LinkedIn Means by “Suggested”

Suggested posts are designed to introduce you to new content.

They typically surface:

  • posts from accounts you don’t follow,
  • topics LinkedIn wants you to discover,
  • content performing well outside your network.

The intention is discovery, not personalization.

Why Suggested Posts Often Feel Irrelevant

Suggestion systems rely on broad signals.

That means they often prioritize:

  • general popularity over personal relevance,
  • exploration over intent,
  • engagement potential over usefulness.

This is why suggested posts frequently miss the context of why you use LinkedIn.

The Accumulated Effect on Your Feed

One suggested post is easy to ignore.

But over time, they:

  • push relevant posts further down,
  • increase scrolling friction,
  • reduce the sense of control.

This effect is part of a broader feed problem: Your Feed Is Your Competitive Advantage

What You Can Do Using Native Controls

LinkedIn offers limited options for suggested posts.

You can:

  • hide individual suggestions,
  • avoid engaging with irrelevant topics,
  • unfollow sources that trigger repeated suggestions.

LinkedIn explains these options here: LinkedIn Help — Hide and mute feed content

These actions help, but they don’t stop suggestions entirely.

Why Manual Filtering Doesn’t Scale

Suggested posts are continuously recalculated.

Even if you hide several today, new ones appear tomorrow.

This limitation is explained in more detail here: How to Clean Your LinkedIn Feed Manually (And Why It Doesn’t Scale)

Suggested posts are designed to broaden what you see.

They are not designed to respect your priorities.

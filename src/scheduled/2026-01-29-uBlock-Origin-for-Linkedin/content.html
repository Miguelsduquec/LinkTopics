What Is uBlock Origin?

uBlock Origin is a free, open-source browser extension designed to block ads, trackers, and unwanted network requests.

It works by applying filter lists that detect known ad patterns across the web.

You can learn more from the official project website: ublockorigin.com

Because LinkedIn serves sponsored content and tracking scripts like many websites, uBlock Origin can block many of them effectively.

What uBlock Origin Does Well on LinkedIn

  • Blocks many sponsored elements (including some promoted blocks and obvious ad containers).
  • Reduces tracking by blocking certain scripts and requests.
  • High performance with low CPU/memory overhead.
  • Advanced customization for power users (rules, filters, allowlists).
  • Free and open-source.

From a pure ad-blocking perspective, uBlock Origin is one of the best tools available.

Why Your LinkedIn Feed Still Feels Noisy

Here’s the key issue:

LinkedIn feed noise is not just ads.

Even if you removed every single ad, your feed can still be filled with:

  • posts liked or commented on by your connections,
  • “Liked by” / “Reacted by” posts from people you don’t follow,
  • suggested posts from outside your network,
  • “Because you viewed…” recommendations,
  • engagement-bait and viral filler.

These are not traditional ads. They’re part of LinkedIn’s feed logic.

Limitations of Using uBlock Origin on LinkedIn

uBlock Origin is an ad blocker. LinkedIn is a behavior-driven social feed.

So naturally, uBlock has limitations on LinkedIn:

  • No feed awareness — it doesn’t understand LinkedIn post types, context, or relevance.
  • No way to remove “Liked by” / “Reacted by” posts reliably.
  • No topic filtering (it can’t keep “tech” and hide “sales”, for example).
  • Suggested content remains because it’s not an “ad container”.
  • No visibility — you don’t get counters or transparency about what was removed.

In other words: uBlock can reduce ads, but it cannot give you feed control.

uBlock Origin vs LinkedIn Feed Cleaners

Ad blockers focus on what is delivered (requests, scripts, ad containers).

Feed cleaners focus on what is shown (post types, structure, relevance).

This distinction matters because LinkedIn’s feed is shaped by engagement, network activity and recommendations — not just ad delivery.

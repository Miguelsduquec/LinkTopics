How to Remove Liked Posts on LinkedIn

Why “Someone liked this” dominates the feed, and what you can realistically do about it.

If your LinkedIn feed feels like it’s mostly other people’s reactions, you’re seeing one of the platform’s strongest distribution mechanics.

Instead of showing you only posts from people you follow, LinkedIn frequently shows posts because someone in your network liked them.

In practice, that turns your feed into a reflection of your network’s engagement — not your priorities.

Why “Liked by” Posts Are Everywhere

LinkedIn is built to maximize engagement. “Liked by” posts are a simple way to expand reach without relying only on followers.

This format works because it:

  • uses social proof (someone you know reacted),
  • surfaces content outside your usual circle,
  • keeps the feed feeling “active” even when people you follow are quiet.

The downside is that relevance becomes harder to maintain — especially for daily users.

How “Network Activity” Hijacks Feed Quality

“Liked by” distribution rewards what your network reacts to most, not what helps you most.

That creates predictable patterns:

  • viral posts repeat across your feed,
  • low-effort content gets boosted through reactions,
  • your feed becomes harder to steer with intent.

If you’ve ever felt like you’re seeing the same type of content on loop, this is a big reason.

The Hidden Cost: Time, Focus, and Missed Signal

Even when a “Liked by” post is harmless, it interrupts the purpose of your feed.

For professionals using LinkedIn to learn, hire, or sell, the cost shows up as:

  • more scrolling to find relevant updates,
  • less trust in what appears “naturally”,
  • more distraction from your actual goals.

This is closely related to the broader problem of engagement-driven ranking: How to Fix the LinkedIn Algorithm

What You Can Do Using LinkedIn’s Native Controls

LinkedIn doesn’t provide a single switch to turn off “Liked by” posts.

But you can reduce their impact by combining several actions:

  • Hide specific posts that are clearly irrelevant,
  • Unfollow noisy connections without disconnecting,
  • Be intentional with engagement (likes and comments train what you see).

LinkedIn documents feed controls like hiding and muting content here: LinkedIn Help — Hide and mute feed content

These steps help, but they require constant attention to maintain.

Why Manual Cleanup Doesn’t Scale

“Liked by” posts are generated by ongoing activity across your network.

So even if you hide one, another appears a few scrolls later.

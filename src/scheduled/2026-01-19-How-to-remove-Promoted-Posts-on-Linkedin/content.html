What Are Promoted Posts on LinkedIn?

Promoted posts are paid ads that appear in your feed and look almost like normal posts. They usually include a small label such as “Promoted” or “Sponsored”.

Advertisers pay LinkedIn to show these posts to you, even if:

  • you don’t follow the company,
  • you’ve never interacted with their content,
  • you’re not interested in the product at all.

From LinkedIn’s perspective, this is normal (ads fund the platform). But from your perspective, promoted posts often:

  • break the flow of useful content,
  • push irrelevant offers,
  • make the feed feel noisy and less professional.

Why You’re Seeing More Promoted Posts in 2026

Promoted posts feel more aggressive than ever for three common reasons:

  1. More sponsored inventory. LinkedIn has more places to insert sponsored content in the feed.
  2. Better targeting. More data = more targeted ads = more ad volume that “fits” your profile (even when you don’t want it).
  3. Blended design. Sponsored posts look more like organic posts, which makes them feel more intrusive.

The result: even if you use LinkedIn for serious, professional reasons, you still get a constant stream of ads and sponsored messages.

Can You Hide Promoted Posts on LinkedIn Using Settings?

LinkedIn does not offer a simple toggle like “Disable promoted posts” or “Hide all sponsored content”.

But you can reduce the noise a little using native controls:

1) Hide individual promoted posts

On a promoted post, click the three-dot menu and choose “I don’t want to see this ad”. If a reason is offered (irrelevant, repetitive, etc.), choose one.

2) Review ad preferences

Go to Settings > Advertising and review your ad preferences. You can reduce certain types of targeting and categories.

3) Unfollow noisy company pages

Some “promoted-looking” content also comes from pages you follow. If you never want to see their posts, unfollowing helps reduce repetition.

These steps help a bit, but they won’t remove all promoted posts.

Why a Generic “LinkedIn Adblock” Extension Usually Fails

If you’ve tried solutions before, you might have used:

  • a browser-wide adblocker,
  • random Chrome extensions that claim “linkedin adblock”,
  • filters designed for other websites.

Sometimes these tools block obvious banners. But LinkedIn promoted posts are different:

  • They’re rendered as feed elements, not classic ad iframes.
  • They use dynamic markup that can change frequently.
  • They mimic organic posts visually, making them harder to target reliably with simple rules.

That’s why generic adblock approaches often break over time: LinkedIn changes the feed; your filters don’t keep up.

uBlock Origin for LinkedIn: Powerful Ad Blocker, Limited Feed Control

Updated for 2026 — why uBlock Origin blocks LinkedIn ads well, but doesn’t fully clean your LinkedIn feed.

uBlock Origin is often the first tool people install when they want to block ads — including on LinkedIn.

And to be fair, it does an excellent job at that.

But many users notice something strange:

Even with uBlock Origin enabled, the LinkedIn feed still feels noisy.

Let’s break down why.

What Is uBlock Origin?

uBlock Origin is a free, open-source browser extension designed to block ads, trackers and unwanted network requests.

It works by applying filter lists that detect known ad patterns across the web.

You can learn more about how it works directly from the official project website: https://ublockorigin.com

Because LinkedIn serves ads like any other website, uBlock Origin can block many of them effectively.

What uBlock Origin Does Well on LinkedIn

  • Blocks many sponsored posts and ads.
  • Reduces tracking scripts and network noise.
  • Highly efficient with minimal performance impact.
  • Advanced customization for power users.
  • Free and open-source.

From a pure ad-blocking perspective, uBlock Origin is one of the best tools available.

Why Your LinkedIn Feed Still Feels Noisy

Here’s the key issue:

LinkedIn feed noise is not just ads.

Even without ads, your feed still includes:

  • posts liked or commented on by your connections,
  • suggested posts from people you don’t follow,
  • “Because you viewed…” recommendations,
  • engagement-driven or viral content.

These elements are part of LinkedIn’s feed logic — not traditional ads.

Limitations of Using uBlock Origin on LinkedIn

  • No understanding of feed structure.
  • No way to remove “Liked by” or “Reacted by” posts.
  • No topic-based filtering.
  • No control over suggested content.
  • No visibility into what’s removed vs what remains.

uBlock Origin treats LinkedIn like a normal website — not a behavior-driven social feed.

uBlock Origin vs LinkedIn Feed Cleaners

Ad blockers focus on what is served.

Feed cleaners focus on what is shown.

This distinction matters because LinkedIn’s algorithm shapes your feed using engagement, network activity and recommendations — not just ads.
